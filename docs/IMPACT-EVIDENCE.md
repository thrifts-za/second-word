# Impact evidence for Second Word

This document supports the problem statement; it does not claim that a survey
proves Second Word will change behaviour. The product must earn that outcome
through a respectful, usable experience and future outcome research.

## Regretted communication is common

- A 2015 **YouGov Omnibus** survey reported that **57% of U.S. social-media
  users** had posted or texted something they later regretted; **16%** said this
  happened at least weekly. Among those who regretted something, hasty replies
  that made the author look foolish were the largest category (36%). The same
  report noted concern about work reputation and family/partner relationships.
  [YouGov source](https://yougov.com/en-us/articles/12854-social-media-blunders-cause-more-damage-important-)
- An **Inc.** article reports results from a **SurveyMonkey survey sponsored by
  On Second Thought** of 1,000 U.S. mobile users: 71% said they had sent a text
  they wished they could take back, 73% realised within seconds, 39% said a bad
  text upset someone close to them, and 16% reported bullying or serious
  professional consequences. It is a sponsored, 2017 survey and should be
  described that way, not treated as an independent population estimate.
  [Inc. source](https://www.inc.com/wanda-thibodeaux/people-want-a-way-to-take-back-bad-texts-this-survey-shows-exactly-why.html)

## What this means for the product

Second Word should not promise to prevent every regret or diagnose a person’s
heart. The evidence motivates a narrow intervention: before sending a message
where something relational is at stake, make room for a voluntary pause,
verified Scripture, and an authored choice. The relevant measures are not
engagement or how often the extension interrupts; they are whether people find
it trustworthy, retain agency, and believe it helped them communicate with more
truth, grace, repair, or gratitude.
